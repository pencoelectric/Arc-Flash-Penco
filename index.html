Section 1

What Is Arc Flash, and Why Does It Matter to Your Facility?

An arc flash is a sudden, violent release of energy caused by an electrical fault. When current jumps through the air between conductors, it creates a plasma arc that can reach temperatures of 35,000 degrees Fahrenheit.. four times hotter than the surface of the sun. The event takes milliseconds. The damage it causes can take years to recover from.

Arc flash events are not rare. The U.S. sees roughly 30,000 incidents per year across industrial and commercial facilities. Most happen during routine tasks: switching gear, testing circuits, working near energized panels. That is not a maintenance problem. It is a program problem.

```
Key point: Arc flash is not just a worker safety issue. It triggers equipment destruction, production downtime, regulatory penalties, and serious civil and criminal liability for the facility and its ownership chain.
🔥

Thermal Energy Release

The plasma arc radiates intense heat in all directions instantly. PPE rated for the task is the only barrier between a worker and third-degree burns or death.

💨

Pressure Wave

The arc vaporizes metal and air, creating a shockwave that can throw workers across a room and collapse unprotected lungs.

⚡

Molten Metal Shrapnel

Copper and aluminum conductors vaporize and become projectiles traveling hundreds of miles per hour, penetrating standard work clothing.

🔴

Acoustic Damage

The sound pressure from a severe arc flash event can cause permanent hearing damage even at a distance, with or without hearing protection.

```
Section 2

NFPA 70B and NFPA 70E: What They Require and Why Both Apply to You

Two NFPA standards govern arc flash and electrical safety in commercial and industrial facilities. Many facility managers know of one. Far fewer have a program built around both. That gap is where liability lives.

```
Standard What It Covers Who It Applies To 2023/2024 Status
NFPA 70B Recommended Practice for Electrical Equipment Maintenance. Frequency, method, and documentation of equipment inspection and testing. Facility owners, property managers, maintenance departments 2023 edition shifted from "recommended" to enforceable language. 2026 revision tightens Section 4.3.3 inspection frequency requirements.
NFPA 70E Standard for Electrical Safety in the Workplace. Safe work practices, PPE requirements, arc flash boundary calculations, training standards. Employers, workers performing electrical tasks, contractors on-site Current edition. Directly supports OSHA 29 CFR 1910.333 compliance. Failure to follow is frequently cited in OSHA General Duty Clause violations.

The 2023 shift in NFPA 70B language is significant. What was once considered advisory guidance is now written in mandatory terms. Inspections are no longer something a facility can postpone without documented justification. If your equipment has not been inspected on the intervals required under 70B, you are already behind the compliance clock.

NFPA 70B Section 4.3.3 establishes condition-based inspection frequencies tied to equipment type and environment. Facilities with high heat, humidity, or heavy cycling equipment face more frequent intervals. The 2026 revision tightens these further. If your maintenance program was built before 2023, it needs a review.

NFPA 70B Condition Classifications

70B assigns equipment a condition rating that drives inspection frequency and urgency of repair. These are not internal grades. They are documented risk classifications.

Condition Definition Required Action
Condition 1 New or like-new. No deficiencies. Operating within normal parameters. Standard inspection intervals. Document and re-inspect on schedule.
Condition 2 Deficiency noted. Equipment is still functional but shows indicators of developing fault. Plan corrective action. Increase inspection frequency. Document timeline.
Condition 3 Critical deficiency. Failure is likely or imminent. Risk of arc flash event is elevated. Immediate corrective action required. De-energize or isolate if possible. Do not defer.
```
Section 3

Arc Flash PPE: The Four Hazard Risk Categories

NFPA 70E establishes four Hazard Risk Categories (HRC) for arc flash PPE, defined by incident energy measured in calories per centimeter squared (cal/cm2). The right PPE level is not a judgment call. It is a calculated requirement based on arc flash hazard analysis of the specific equipment being worked on.

Working at the wrong HRC level is not a policy violation. It is an exposure that can kill. A worker in HRC 1 gear on an HRC 4 task is effectively unprotected.

```
HRC 1
Up to 4 cal/cm2
Arc-rated shirt and pants or coverall. Face shield. Safety glasses.
HRC 2
Up to 8 cal/cm2
Arc-rated shirt and pants, arc flash suit hood. Hearing protection added.
HRC 3
Up to 25 cal/cm2
Arc flash suit, jacket, and pants. Full balaclava. Heavy-duty gloves.
HRC 4
Up to 40 cal/cm2
Maximum arc flash suit system. Full head, face, hand, and body coverage rated for 40 cal.
Important: PPE category alone does not constitute a safe work program. NFPA 70E requires arc flash boundaries to be established, approach distances respected, and equipment-specific hazard analysis documented. PPE without a program is compliance theater.
```
Section 4

How Infrared Thermography and Ultrasonic Inspection Detect Faults Before They Become Incidents

Both NFPA 70B and 70E support predictive maintenance inspection as a primary method for identifying electrical hazards before they cause an event. Two technologies lead this work: infrared (IR) thermography and ultrasonic testing (UT).

These are not optional enhancements. Under the 2023 and anticipated 2026 revisions to 70B, documented inspection with results tied to corrective action timelines is the expectation. "We had someone look at it" does not meet the standard.

```
🌡

Infrared Thermography

IR cameras detect heat signatures from loose connections, overloaded circuits, failing components, and unbalanced loads. Anomalies invisible to the eye become visible patterns. NFPA 70B Section 7.4 requires delta-T documentation. Severity is classified by temperature differential, driving corrective action timelines.

🔴

Ultrasonic Inspection

Ultrasonic testing identifies partial discharge, corona, and arcing inside switchgear and transformers. These events often precede catastrophic failure by weeks or months. UT detects what IR cannot: electrical discharge activity inside sealed equipment that produces no external heat signature yet.

The combination of IR and UT gives a facility the most complete picture of electrical health available without taking equipment offline. Together they cover thermal anomalies, mechanical looseness, and developing electrical discharge.. the three primary precursors to arc flash events in commercial and industrial equipment.

```
Section 5

Liability Exposure: What Happens When an Incident Occurs Without a Documented Safety Program

The arc flash event is the moment everything changes. What existed before it. or didn't. determines whether the facility and its ownership survive what comes next.

```

OSHA General Duty Clause

Under Section 5(a)(1), employers must provide a workplace free from recognized hazards. Arc flash is a recognized hazard. OSHA does not need a specific arc flash regulation to cite you. No program, no documentation, no defense. Penalties per violation now reach $16,550 for serious citations and $165,514 for willful or repeated violations.

Civil Litigation

Workers injured in arc flash incidents, or their families, routinely pursue civil action against facility owners and operators. Without documented inspection records, corrective action plans, and PPE protocols, the facility has no evidence of due diligence. Settlements and verdicts routinely exceed $5M. Some have exceeded $30M.

Criminal Exposure

In cases involving fatalities or severe injuries where willful neglect can be demonstrated, facility managers and executives have faced criminal prosecution under OSHA's willful violation provisions. Documented awareness of a hazard without corrective action is the core of that case. Your maintenance records are evidence.

Insurance Consequences

Post-incident, insurers review maintenance records and compliance documentation before paying claims. Facilities without 70B-aligned inspection programs face claim denial, premium increases, or policy cancellation. The cost of a compliant program is a fraction of what a denied claim costs.

Contractor Liability

If a contractor or service vendor is injured at your facility due to undisclosed arc flash hazards or failure to provide hazard analysis documentation, the facility owner shares liability. NFPA 70E requires host employers to document hazards and communicate them to all workers on-site, including third parties.

Regulatory Cascades

An OSHA inspection triggered by an incident rarely stops at the incident itself. Inspectors examine the broader program. One gap becomes ten citations. Facilities with no 70B maintenance documentation and no 70E training records face compounding violations across multiple standards simultaneously.

The compliance clock is running whether or not you've started the program. NFPA 70B's 2023 language shift means the question is no longer "should we do this?" It's "how far behind are we?" A documented inspection with findings and a corrective action timeline is the starting point for defensibility. Without it, the facility has no story to tell.
```
Section 6

What a Qualified Electrical Safety Program Actually Looks Like

A compliant electrical safety program aligned to NFPA 70B and 70E is not a binder on a shelf. It is a living system with inspection cycles, documentation, corrective action tracking, training records, and vendor accountability. Here is what it includes.

```
📄

Arc Flash Hazard Analysis

A documented study of each piece of electrical equipment identifying incident energy levels, arc flash boundaries, and required PPE categories. Required by NFPA 70E before energized work begins.

🔍

Scheduled Inspection Program

IR thermography and ultrasonic inspection on 70B-aligned intervals, with findings documented, classified by condition, and tied to corrective action timelines.

🔧

Deficiency Remediation Tracking

Every Condition 2 and Condition 3 finding requires a documented corrective action plan with assigned responsibility and completion date. Findings without remediation records are open liability.

🎓

Qualified Worker Training

NFPA 70E requires that workers performing or supervising electrical tasks be trained as "qualified persons." Training must be documented and refreshed on a defined cycle.

🔒

Lockout / Tagout Integration

LOTO procedures must be aligned with arc flash hazard analysis. Equipment-specific LOTO documentation reduces both arc flash exposure and OSHA citation risk simultaneously.

📋

Vendor Accountability Standards

Any contractor performing electrical work at your facility must operate under a compliant safety program. The host employer is responsible for ensuring third parties meet 70E requirements on-site.
