Section 1

What Is Arc Flash, and Why Does It Matter to Your Facility?

An arc flash is a sudden, violent release of energy caused by an electrical fault. When current jumps through the air between conductors, it creates a plasma arc that can reach temperatures of 35,000 degrees Fahrenheit.. four times hotter than the surface of the sun. The event takes milliseconds. The damage it causes can take years to recover from.

Arc flash events are not rare. The U.S. sees roughly 30,000 incidents per year across industrial and commercial facilities. Most happen during routine tasks: switching gear, testing circuits, working near energized panels. That is not a maintenance problem. It is a program problem.

Key point: Arc flash is not just a worker safety issue. It triggers equipment destruction, production downtime, regulatory penalties, and serious civil and criminal liability for the facility and its ownership chain.
🔥

Thermal Energy Release

The plasma arc radiates intense heat in all directions instantly. PPE rated for the task is the only barrier between a worker and third-degree burns or death.

💨

Pressure Wave

The arc vaporizes metal and air, creating a shockwave that can throw workers across a room and collapse unprotected lungs.

⚡

Molten Metal Shrapnel

Copper and aluminum conductors vaporize and become projectiles traveling hundreds of miles per hour, penetrating standard work clothing.

🔴

Acoustic Damage

The sound pressure from a severe arc flash event can cause permanent hearing damage even at a distance, with or without hearing protection.

Section 2

NFPA 70B and NFPA 70E: What They Require and Why Both Apply to You

Two NFPA standards govern arc flash and electrical safety in commercial and industrial facilities. Many facility managers know of one. Far fewer have a program built around both. That gap is where liability lives.

StandardWhat It CoversWho It Applies To2023/2024 Status
NFPA 70BRecommended Practice for Electrical Equipment Maintenance. The 2023 shift in NFPA 70B language is significant. What was once considered advisory guidance is now written in mandatory terms. If your equipment has not been inspected on the intervals required under 70B, you are already behind the compliance clock.

NFPA 70B Section 4.3.3 establishes condition-based inspection frequencies tied to equipment type and environment. The 2026 revision tightens these further. If your maintenance program was built before 2023, it needs a review.

NFPA 70B Condition Classifications

70B assigns equipment a condition rating that drives inspection frequency and urgency of repair.

Section 3

Arc Flash PPE: The Four Hazard Risk Categories

NFPA 70E establishes four Hazard Risk Categories for arc flash PPE, defined by incident energy measured in calories per centimeter squared. The right PPE level is a calculated requirement based on arc flash hazard analysis of the specific equipment being worked on.

Working at the wrong HRC level is not a policy violation. It is an exposure that can kill.

HRC 1
Up to 4 cal/cm2
Arc-rated shirt and pants or coverall. Face shield. Safety glasses.
HRC 2
Up to 8 cal/cm2
Arc-rated shirt and pants, arc flash suit hood. Hearing protection added.
HRC 3
Up to 25 cal/cm2
Arc flash suit, jacket, and pants. Full balaclava. Heavy-duty gloves.
HRC 4
Up to 40 cal/cm2
Maximum arc flash suit system. Full head, face, hand, and body coverage rated for 40 cal.
Important: PPE category alone does not constitute a safe work program. NFPA 70E requires arc flash boundaries to be established, approach distances respected, and equipment-specific hazard analysis documented.
Section 4

How Infrared Thermography and Ultrasonic Inspection Detect Faults Before They Become Incidents

Both NFPA 70B and 70E support predictive maintenance inspection as a primary method for identifying electrical hazards before they cause an event. Two technologies lead this work: infrared thermography and ultrasonic testing.

🌡

Infrared Thermography

IR cameras detect heat signatures from loose connections, overloaded circuits, failing components, and unbalanced loads. NFPA 70B Section 7.4 requires delta-T documentation. Severity is classified by temperature differential, driving corrective action timelines.

🔴

Ultrasonic Inspection

Ultrasonic testing identifies partial discharge, corona, and arcing inside switchgear and transformers. These events often precede catastrophic failure by weeks or months. UT detects what IR cannot: electrical discharge activity inside sealed equipment.

The combination of IR and UT gives a facility the most complete picture of electrical health available without taking equipment offline.

Section 5

Liability Exposure: What Happens When an Incident Occurs Without a Documented Safety Program

The arc flash event is the moment everything changes. What existed before it. or didn't. determines whether the facility and its ownership survive what comes next.

OSHA General Duty Clause

Under Section 5(a)(1), employers must provide a workplace free from recognized hazards. Arc flash is a recognized hazard. No program, no documentation, no defense. Penalties per violation now reach $16,550 for serious citations and $165,514 for willful or repeated violations.

Civil Litigation

Workers injured in arc flash incidents, or their families, routinely pursue civil action against facility owners and operators. Settlements and verdicts routinely exceed $5M. Some have exceeded $30M.

Criminal Exposure

In cases involving fatalities or severe injuries where willful neglect can be demonstrated, facility managers and executives have faced criminal prosecution. Documented awareness of a hazard without corrective action is the core of that case.

Insurance Consequences

Post-incident, insurers review maintenance records and compliance documentation before paying claims. Facilities without 70B-aligned inspection programs face claim denial, premium increases, or policy cancellation.

Contractor Liability

If a contractor or service vendor is injured at your facility due to undisclosed arc flash hazards, the facility owner shares liability. NFPA 70E requires host employers to document hazards and communicate them to all workers on-site.

Regulatory Cascades

An OSHA inspection triggered by an incident rarely stops at the incident itself. One gap becomes ten citations. Facilities with no 70B maintenance documentation and no 70E training records face compounding violations.

The compliance clock is running whether or not you have started the program. A documented inspection with findings and a corrective action timeline is the starting point for defensibility. Without it, the facility has no story to tell.
