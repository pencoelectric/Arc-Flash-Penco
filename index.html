Section 1

What Is Arc Flash?

An arc flash is a violent release of electrical energy that creates a plasma arc reaching 35,000 degrees Fahrenheit.. four times hotter than the sun's surface. It happens in milliseconds. The U.S. sees 30,000 incidents per year, most during routine tasks like switching gear or testing circuits. That is not a maintenance problem. It is a program problem.

Thermal Energy

Plasma arc radiates lethal heat in all directions. Rated PPE is the only barrier between a worker and fatal burns.

Pressure Wave

The shockwave vaporizes metal and air, throwing workers across rooms and collapsing unprotected lungs.

Molten Shrapnel

Copper and aluminum conductors vaporize into projectiles traveling hundreds of miles per hour.

Acoustic Damage

Sound pressure from a severe event causes permanent hearing damage even at a distance.

Arc flash triggers equipment destruction, production downtime, regulatory penalties, and serious civil and criminal liability for the facility and its entire ownership chain.

Section 2

Two Standards. Most Facilities Know One.

Two NFPA standards govern electrical safety and maintenance in commercial and industrial facilities. NFPA 70B covers equipment maintenance: inspection frequency, method, and documentation. NFPA 70E covers electrical safety in the workplace: PPE, arc flash boundaries, and safe work practices.

Most facility managers are familiar with one of these standards. Some are familiar with both. Very few are actually compliant with either. The gap between awareness and compliance is where liability lives, and it is wider than most organizations realize.

NFPA 70B became enforceable in 2023. The 2026 revision tightens requirements further. NFPA 70E supports OSHA 29 CFR 1910.333 and is frequently cited in General Duty Clause violations. Together, these two standards create a compliance framework that covers both the equipment and the people working on or near it.

If your electrical maintenance program was built before 2023, it was built to a standard that no longer exists. The compliance clock is already running.
Section 3

Liability Exposure: What Happens Without a Documented Safety Program

The arc flash event is the moment everything changes. What existed before it.. or didn't.. determines whether the facility survives what comes next.

OSHA General Duty Clause

Section 5(a)(1) requires workplaces free from recognized hazards. No program, no documentation, no defense. Penalties reach $16,550 per serious violation and $165,514 for willful or repeated citations.

Civil Litigation

Injured workers or families routinely pursue civil action. Without inspection records, corrective action plans, and PPE protocols, the facility has no evidence of due diligence. Verdicts routinely exceed $5M. Some have exceeded $30M.

Criminal Exposure

In fatality cases with documented willful neglect, facility managers and executives have faced criminal prosecution. Awareness of a hazard without corrective action is the core of that case. Your maintenance records are the evidence.

Insurance Consequences

Insurers review maintenance records before paying claims. Facilities without inspection programs aligned to current standards face claim denial, premium increases, or cancellation. The cost of a compliant program is a fraction of a denied claim.

Contractor Liability

If a contractor is injured due to undisclosed arc flash hazards, the facility owner shares liability. NFPA 70E requires host employers to document and communicate hazards to all workers on-site, including third parties.

Regulatory Cascades

An OSHA inspection triggered by one incident rarely stops there. One gap becomes ten citations. Facilities without maintenance documentation and training records face compounding violations across multiple standards simultaneously.

Section 4

After the Incident: The Next 12 Months

An arc flash event does not end when the fire goes out. It triggers a cascading sequence of investigations, audits, and legal exposure that can take years to resolve and cost multiples of the original damage. Here is what the timeline actually looks like.

HOUR 1

Incident Response

Emergency services respond. Injured workers transported. Equipment isolated. OSHA must be notified within 8 hours for a hospitalization, within 24 hours for an amputation or loss of an eye. A fatality requires notification within 8 hours.

WEEK 1

OSHA Investigation Opens

An inspector arrives at the facility. They request maintenance records, training documentation, arc flash hazard analysis, PPE logs, and LOTO procedures. If those records don't exist, the investigation expands. What started as one incident becomes a full-facility audit.

MONTH 1-3

Citations and Legal Exposure

OSHA issues citations. Serious violations carry penalties up to $16,550 each. Willful violations reach $165,514. Simultaneously, the injured worker or their family retains legal counsel. Discovery begins. Every maintenance decision you made or didn't make becomes evidence.

MONTH 3-6

Insurance Review

Your insurer reviews the claim. They request the same documentation OSHA did. Without a maintenance program aligned to current NFPA standards, the claim is at risk. Denial, exclusion, or reduced payout are all on the table. Premiums are repriced regardless of outcome.

MONTH 6-12

Civil Litigation and Settlement

Depositions. Expert witnesses. Document production. The facility's maintenance history is the central exhibit. Facilities without documented inspection programs, corrective action tracking, and training records have no defense narrative. Verdicts in arc flash cases routinely exceed $5M.

YEAR 1+

Ongoing Consequences

Criminal referral in fatality cases. Increased insurance premiums for 3-5 years. Contractor reluctance to work at the facility. Customer and tenant scrutiny. Regulatory follow-up inspections. The incident becomes the defining event in the facility's operating history.

Section 5

The Questions Your Facility Cannot Afford to Leave Unanswered

Most facilities believe they are covered. When the incident happens, they discover they are not. The difference between a defensible position and an indefensible one comes down to whether your organization can answer these questions with documentation, not memory.

1
When was your last documented electrical inspection, and what standard was it performed to?
2
Can you produce condition classifications for every piece of critical electrical equipment in your facility?
3
Do you have a corrective action plan with assigned responsibility and completion dates for every known deficiency?
4
Are your arc flash hazard labels current, and do they reflect the actual incident energy levels at each piece of equipment?
5
Can you document that every worker performing or supervising electrical tasks has been trained as a qualified person under NFPA 70E?
6
If a contractor is injured at your facility tomorrow, can you prove you communicated all known electrical hazards before they started work?
7
Does your insurer have a copy of your current electrical maintenance program on file?
8
If OSHA walked in today, how many of these answers would you have in writing?
If you cannot answer every one of these questions with a documented record,

your facility is carrying risk that compounds every day it goes unaddressed. The cost of finding out is a fraction of the cost of finding out too late.
